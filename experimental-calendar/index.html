Chernobyl disaster still remains TODAY

This is a record of the proportion of radioactive elements remaining in the area of the Chernobyl nuclear power plant after the Chernobyl disaster. It will reflect the degree to which the four major radioactive elements Iodine-131, Cesium-137, Strontium-90, and Plutonium-239 decay over time. The Chernobyl disaster, which occurred on April 26, 1986, is one of the most catastrophic nuclear accidents in history. Located in the then-Soviet territory of Ukraine, the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant experienced a massive explosion in its Reactor No. 4. The explosion released large quantities of radioactive particles into the atmosphere, which then spread over a vast area of Europe.

The immediate cause of the accident was a sudden power output surge during a reactor systems test. Flawed reactor technology coupled with inadequately trained personnel exacerbated the situation, leading to the explosion and subsequent fires. Over the following weeks, emergency crews worked tirelessly to contain the fires and prevent further explosions.

Immediate health effects included two plant workers dying on the day of the explosion, with 28 more people succumbing to acute radiation sickness in the subsequent days. However, the long-term health impacts, including increased cancer risks, affected thousands. Large areas around Chernobyl remain uninhabitable due to lingering radioactive contamination. The disaster not only impacted health and the environment but also significantly influenced nuclear policies worldwide.

Iodine-131 (I-131): With a half-life of 8 days, this short-lived radioactive isotope posed the most immediate threat to humans and the environment post-accident. I-131 can be inhaled or ingested, accumulating in the human thyroid, thereby increasing the risk of thyroid cancer. Due to its brief half-life, it has largely decayed by now.

Cesium-137 (Cs-137): Possessing a half-life of 30 years, this isotope is one of the primary long-lasting radioactive contaminants in the environment post-disaster. Its half-life suggests that by 2021, 35 years after the accident, its initial amount has reduced to about half.

Strontium-90 (Sr-90): With a half-life of 28.8 years, its decay pattern is similar to that of Cs-137, implying that its residual environmental presence has also approximately halved by now.

Plutonium-239 (Pu-239): This isotope has a staggering half-life of 24,100 years. As a long-lived radioactive element, it continues to linger in the soils of the Chernobyl region.
